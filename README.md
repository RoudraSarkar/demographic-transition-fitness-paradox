# dissertation-
This repository accompanies my MSc dissertation in Applied Social Data Science at Trinity College Dublin. The project investigates the demographic transition fitness paradox: across most of human history, wealth and reproductive success moved together; in modern populations the relationship has flattened or reversed, with the most educated and highest-earning individuals having the fewest children. This contradicts the central prediction of human behavioural ecology and remains an open theoretical puzzle. Using education-stratified fertility data from the Wittgenstein Centre, attitudinal data from the European Social Survey, country-level covariates from V-Dem and the OECD, and individual-level data from the 1970 British Cohort Study, I test four hypotheses about the shape, timing, and mechanisms of the socioeconomic-fertility gradient across European cohorts born 1930–1980. Methods combine panel econometrics, quantile regression, interpretable machine learning, Bayesian hierarchical modelling, and causal mediation analysis.
